Infinite-Horizon Problems

Transversality Conditions

The Variational View

Recalling
$$
\mathscr{V}=\underbrace{\int^T_0\left[H(t,y,u,\lambda)+y(t)\dot{\lambda}\right]dt}_{\Omega_1}-\underbrace{\lambda(T)y_T}_{\Omega_2}+\underbrace{\lambda(0)y_0}_{\Omega_3}\tag{7.22’}
$$

  • Generating neighboring control paths and state paths

$$
u(t)=u^*(T)+\epsilon p(t) \tag{7.24}
$$

$$
y(t)=y^*(T)+\epsilon q(t) \tag{7.25}
$$

  • If $T$ and $y_T$ are variable, we also have
    $$
    T=T^*+\epsilon\Delta T\ \ \ \mbox{and}\ \ \ \ y_T=y_T^*+\epsilon\Delta y_T\tag{7.26}
    $$

  • And the necessary condition
    $$
    \frac{d\mathscr{V}}{d\epsilon}=\int^{T(\epsilon)}0\left{\left[\left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial y}+\dot{\lambda}\right)q(t)+\frac{\partial H}{\partial u}p(t)\right]\right}dt+[H]{t=T}\Delta T-\lambda(T)\Delta y_{T}=0\tag{7.30}
    $$

When adapted to the infinite-horizon framework, (7.30) becomes
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d\mathscr{V}}{d\epsilon}&=\underbrace{\int^{\infty}0\left{\left[\left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial y}+\dot{\lambda}\right)q(t)+\frac{\partial H}{\partial u}p(t)\right]\right}dt}{\Omega_1}+\underbrace{\lim_{t\to\infty}[H]{t=T}\Delta T}{\Omega_2}-\underbrace{\lim_{t\to\infty}\lambda(T)\Delta y_{T}}_{\Omega_3}\&=0
\end{aligned}\tag{9.6}
$$

  • Similarly, three components must be set equal to zero individually.

  • The vanishing of $\Omega_1$ gives us
    $$
    \dot{\lambda}=-\frac{\partial H}{\partial y}\ \ \ \mbox{ [Equation of Motion for }\lambda ]
    $$

    $$
    \frac{\partial H}{\partial u}=0\ \ \ \mbox{ [Weaker version of }\max_uH \mbox{ condition} ]
    $$

The Variability of $T$ and $y_T$

  • The vanishing of $\Omega_2$ gives us
    $$
    \lim_{t\to\infty}H=0\ \ \ \ \ \ \mbox{ [Infinite-Horizon Transversality Condition]}\tag{9.7}
    $$

    • which plays a central role of a general transversality condition for infinite-horizon problems, regardless of the terminal state is fixed.

  • Suppose the terminal state is fixed, then we have $\lim_{t\to\infty}y-T=y_\infty$, which implies $\Delta y_T=0$ at time infinite, the $\Omega_3$ term will vanish without requiring any restriction on the terminal value of $\lambda$.

  • With a free terminal state, the vanishing of $\Omega_3$ gives us
    $$
    \lim_{t\to\infty}\lambda(t)=0\ \ \ \ \ \ \mbox{ [Infinite-Horizon Transversality Condition for a Free Terminal State]}\tag{9.4}
    $$

  • Similarly, in case the variable terminal state is subject to a prespecified minimum level $y_{\min}$ as $t\to\infty$, we may expect the infinite-horizon transversality condition to be
    $$
    \lim_{t\to\infty}\lambda(t)\geq0\ \ \ \ \ \ and\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \lim_{t\to\infty}\lambda(t)[y(t)-y_{min}]=0\tag{9.5}
    $$

The Neoclassical Theory of Optimal Growth

The Model

The neoclassical theory of optimal growth extends the Ramsey model in two major respects:

  1. The labor force (identified with the population) is assumed to be growing at an exogenous constant rate $n > 0$ (the Ramsey model has $n = 0$), and
  2. the social utility is assumed to be subject to time discounting at a constant rate $\rho > 0$ (the Ramsey model has $\rho=0$).

This theory is labeled a “neoclassical” theory, because its analytical framework revolves around the neoclassical production function $Y = Y(K, L)$, assumed to be characterized by constant returns to scale, positive marginal products, and diminishing returns to each input.

  • Such a production function, being linearly homogeneous, can be rewritten in per-worker terms -or per-capita terms, as we shall draw no distinction between the population and the labor force.
    $$
    \begin{array}{ll}
    y\equiv \frac{Y}{L}&\mbox{average product of labor}\\
    \\
    k\equiv\frac{K}{L}&\mbox{capital-labor ratio}
    \end{array}
    $$

  • So we express the production function as
    $$
    y=\phi(k)\ \ \ \ \mbox{with}\ \phi’(k)>0\mbox{ and}\ \phi’’(k)<0\ \ \ \ \mbox{for all} k>0\tag{9.22}
    $$

  • Additionally, it is stipulated that
    $$
    \lim_{k\to 0}\phi’(k)=\infty,\ \ \ and\ \ \ \lim_{k\to\infty}\phi’(k)=0
    $$

  • The net investment, $I=\dot{K}$ can be expressed as
    $$
    \dot{K}=I_g-\delta K=Y-C-\delta K
    $$

  • Using the symbol $c\equiv \frac{C}{L}$ for per-capita consumption, we have
    $$
    \frac{\dot{K}}{L}=y-c-\delta k=\phi(k)-c-\delta k\tag{9.23}
    $$

  • Turning the left-hand side into per-capita variables
    $$
    \begin{aligned}

    \dot{K}&\equiv\frac{dK}{dt}=\frac{d}{dt}(kL)=k\frac{dL}{dt}+L\frac{dk}{dt}\\
&=knL+L\dot{k}\\
&=L(kn+\dot{k})
\end{aligned}

    $$

  • Rearranging we have
    $$
    \dot{k}=\phi(k)-c-(n+\delta)k\tag{9.24}
    $$

The utility function possess the following properties:
$$
\begin{aligned}
&U’(c)>0\ and\ U’’(c)<0\ \ \ \ \ for\ all\ c>0\ \ &\lim_{c\to 0}U’(c)=\infty,\ \ \ and\ \ \ \lim_{c\to\infty}U’(c)=0
\end{aligned}\tag{9.25}
$$

With a discount rate $\rho$ the objective function takes the form
$$
\begin{aligned}
\int^\infty_0 U(c)L(t)e^{-\rho t}dt&=\int^\infty_0U(c)L_0e^{nt}e^{-\rho t}dt
\
&=L_0\int^\infty_0U(c)e^{-(\rho-n)t}dt
\end{aligned}\tag{9.26}
$$

  • To ensure convergence, we have $\rho-n>0$, let $r=\rho-n$, and set $L_0=1$ by choice of unit, the functional will reduce to
    $$
    \int^\infty_0U(c)e^{-rt}dt\tag{9.26’}
    $$

The problem of optima growth is
$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\mbox{Maximize}& \int^\infty_0U(c)e^{-rt}dt\
\
\mbox{subject to}& \dot{k}=\phi(k)-c-(n+\delta)k\
\
& k(0)=k_0\
\
\mbox{and}&0\leq c(t)\leq\phi[k(t)]
\end{array}\tag{9.27}
$$

The Maximum Principle

The Hamiltonian
$$
H=U(c)e^{-rt}+\lambda[\phi(k)-c-(n+\delta)k]\tag{9.28}
$$

  • $H$ contains a hump with its peak occurring at a $c$ value between $c=0$ and $c=c_1$.

    • Since $c_1$ is the solution of the equation $[\phi(k)-c-(n+\delta)k]=0$, it follows that $c_1=\phi(k)-(n+\delta)k$, so that $c_1<\phi(k)$.
    • Hence, the maximum of $H$ corresponds to a value of $c$ in the interior of the control region $[0,\phi(k)]$.
    • We can accordingly find the maximum of $H$ by setting

    $$
    \frac{\partial H}{\partial c}=U’(c)e^{-rt}-\lambda=0
    $$

  • From this, we obtain the condition
    $$
    U’(c)=\lambda e^{rt}\tag{9.29}
    $$

    • which states that, optimally, the marginal utility of per-capita consumption should be equal to the shadow price of capital amplified by the exponential term $e^{rt}$.

  • Since $\frac{\partial^2 H}{\partial c^2}=U’’(c)e^{-rt}$ is negative by (9.25)​, $H$ is indeed maximized.

The maximum principle calls for two equations of motion.

  • $\dot{\lambda}=-\frac{\partial H}{\partial k}$ implies
    $$
    \dot{\lambda}=-\lambda[\phi’(k)-(n+\delta)]\tag{9.30}
    $$

  • $\dot{k}=\frac{\partial H}{\partial \lambda}$ implies
    $$
    \dot{k}=\phi(k)-c-(n+\delta)k\tag{9.31}
    $$

The three equations (9.29)​ through (9.31)​ should in principle enable us to solve for the three variables $c$, $A$, and $k$.

  • Without knowledge of the specific forms of the $U(c)$ and $\phi(k)$ functions, however, we can only undertake a qualitative analysis of the model.
